Core Concepts

The 2-Step Deletion Rule

Because Splay Trees are a variant of Binary Search Trees (BST), we start with standard BST rules, but add one critical balancing step at the end.

  • Step 1: Delete. Perform a standard BST deletion on the target node.
  • Step 2: Splay. After deletion, you MUST splay the Parent of the node you just deleted. Keep splaying until that parent becomes the new Root of the tree.

Instructor's Golden Rules:

👉 What if the node isn't in the tree? Splay the last accessed non-null node.
👉 What if you delete the Root? The root has no parent, so no splaying is required!

Starting Splay Tree
Example 1: Leaf Node

Deleting 12

Step 1: Search & Delete
We trace down the tree: 14 → 13 → 7 → 10 → 12. Because 12 is a leaf node (0 children), we simply delete it. The parent of 12 is 10.

Step 2: Splay the Parent (10)
We must bring 10 to the root.

  • Zig-Zag Rotation: 10 is the right child of 7, and 7 is the left child of 13. We perform a Left rotation on 7, then a Right rotation on 13.
  • Zig Rotation: 10 is now the left child of 14 (the root). We perform a Right rotation on 14 to make 10 the new root.
graph TD 10((10)) --- 7((7)) 10 --- 14((14)) 14 --- 13((13)) 14 --- 16((16)) 16 --- 15((15)) 16 --- 17((17))
Tree after deleting 12 and splaying 10 to the root.
Example 2: Node with 2 Children

Deleting 14

Step 1: Search & Delete
We find 14 (currently the right child of 10). Because 14 has two children (13 and 16), standard BST rules state we must replace it with its In-order Successor (the smallest element in its right subtree).

The right subtree starts at 16, and the smallest element there is 15. We replace 14 with 15, and delete the original leaf node 15.

Step 2: Splay the Parent
The original position of the node we wanted to delete (14) had the parent 10. We must splay 10. However, 10 is already the root! Therefore, no splaying rotations are needed.

15 replaces 14. 10 is already the root, so no splay occurs.
Example 3: Node with 1 Child

Deleting 16

Step 1: Search & Delete
We find 16. It has only one child (17). By BST rules, we delete 16 and connect its only child (17) directly to 16's parent (15).

Step 2: Splay the Parent (15)
The parent of the deleted node (16) was 15. We must bring 15 to the root.

  • Zag Rotation: 15 is the right child of the root (10). This requires a single Left Rotation on 10.
  • 15 becomes the root, 10 drops down to become its left child, and 13 is handed over to be the right child of 10.
graph TD 15((15)) --- 10((10)) 15 --- 17((17)) 10 --- 7((7)) 10 --- 13((13))
Tree after deleting 16 and splaying 15 to the root.
Edge Case: Not Found

Attempting to Delete 20

Step 1: Search & Delete
We search for 20: 20 > 15 (go right to 17), 20 > 17 (go right... NULL). 20 is not in the tree, so we delete nothing.

Step 2: Splay the Last Accessed Node
The instructor's rule: if a node is not found, you must splay the last non-null node you touched. We touched 17 last.

  • Zag Rotation: 17 is the right child of 15. We perform a Left Rotation on 15 to bring 17 to the root.

Edge Case: Root Node

Deleting 17 (The Root)

Step 1: Search & Delete
We want to delete 17, which is currently the root of the tree. It has one child (the left subtree starting at 15). We delete 17, and 15 moves up to become the new root.

Step 2: Splay the Parent
Because 17 was the root, it had no parent. Therefore, the deletion process is complete and no splaying is required!

Lecture Conclusion

The instructor concludes by defining this specific methodology as the "Bottom-Up Splaying Approach" for deletion.

If an exam or assignment specifically asks for Bottom-Up Deletion, this is the exact two-step framework you use. In the next lecture, the instructor will cover the alternative method: the Top-Down Approach, which involves splitting the tree into two parts and merging them together.
