1. What is an Expression?

An expression is a combination of constants, variables, symbols, operators, and parentheses arranged according to grammar rules. In this lesson, we specifically focus on binary operators (operators that require exactly two operands).

The Standard: Infix Notation

This is the standard mathematical format humans use. The operator sits in between the operands.

<operand> <operator> <operand>

Crucial Concept: Operands are not always just simple numbers. They can be entire complex sub-expressions themselves!

(a - 1) + 5

Here, the entire expression (a-1) acts as Operand 1 for the + operator.

2. The Problem with Infix

Because Infix expressions place the operator in the middle, they can be highly ambiguous to computers. Look at the expression below:

5 + 1 * 6

How do we evaluate this? There are two ways to read it:

  • Wrong Way (Left to Right): 5 + 1 = 6. Then 6 * 6 = 36.
  • Correct Way (Precedence): 1 * 6 = 6. Then 5 + 6 = 11.
Why computers hate this: Parsing Infix expressions is easy for humans but requires a lot of extra time and memory for a computer. The computer has to constantly scan back and forth to check precedence, associativity, and parentheses.

3. The Rules: Precedence & Associativity

To get the correct answer from an Infix expression, we must follow strict grammar rules.

  • Precedence: Dictates which operation happens first.
  • Associativity: Acts as a tie-breaker when two operators in the expression have the exact same precedence level.
Priority Operator(s) Name Associativity
1 (Highest) ( ) [ ] { } Parentheses / Brackets N/A
2 ^ Exponential (Power) Right to Left (R → L)
3 * / Multiply / Divide Left to Right (L → R)
4 (Lowest) + - Add / Subtract Left to Right (L → R)

4. Step-by-Step: Left-to-Right Associativity

Let's evaluate a complex expression where operators tie for precedence, forcing us to use Left to Right associativity.

Original: 1 + 2 * 5 + 30 / 5 Step 1: 1 + [2 * 5] + 30 / 5 (* and / tie. Read L->R. Do * first) Step 2: 1 + 10 + 30 / 5 Step 3: 1 + 10 + [30 / 5] (Now do the division) Step 4: 1 + 10 + 6 Step 5: [1 + 10] + 6 (+ and + tie. Read L->R. Do first +) Answer: 17

5. Step-by-Step: Right-to-Left Associativity

Exponentials (Powers) are unique because their associativity goes Right to Left. Look at how this changes the math entirely!

Original: 2 ^ 2 ^ 3 Step 1: 2 ^ [2 ^ 3] (^ and ^ tie. Read R->L. Do rightmost first!) Step 2: 2 ^ 8 Answer: 256
Warning: If you evaluated this Left to Right, you would do (2^2) = 4, then 4^3 = 64. This is computationally incorrect!

6. The Solution: Prefix Notation

Also known as Polish Notation. By moving the operator to the front of the operands, we eliminate the need for parentheses and precedence rules entirely!

<operator> <operand> <operand>

Visualizing the Conversion

Let's convert the Infix expression a * b + c to Prefix.

Infix: a * b + c Step 1: Observe precedence. * happens before +. Step 2: [* a b] + c (Convert a*b first. Treat it as a single operand) Step 3: + [* a b] c (Move the + to the front of the two operands) Prefix: + * a b c

7. The Solution: Postfix Notation

Also known as Reverse Polish Notation. The operator is moved to the end of the operands. This is highly preferred by computer Stack architectures.

<operand> <operand> <operator>

Visualizing the Conversion

Let's convert the Infix expression a * b + c to Postfix.

Infix: a * b + c Step 1: Observe precedence. * happens before +. Step 2: [a b *] + c (Convert a*b first. Treat it as a single operand) Step 3: [a b *] c + (Move the + to the end of the two operands) Postfix: a b * c +

8. Summary Conclusion

Infix, Prefix, and Postfix are three equivalent ways to write the exact same math problem.

  • Infix is best for humans. It requires Precedence and Associativity rules to be understood.
  • Prefix and Postfix are best for computers. A computer can parse them linearly (Left-to-Right or Right-to-Left) using a Stack data structure without ever worrying about BEDMAS/PEMDAS rules or parentheses. This saves time and memory!
