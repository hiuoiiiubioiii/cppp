Prim's Algorithm

Instructor: Jenny | Finding the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST)

Introduction

Goal: To find the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) from a given weighted, undirected graph.

Context: This follows Kruskal's Algorithm. Both solve the same problem but use different logic:

  • Kruskal's Approach: Sorts all edges globally by weight and adds them sequentially, skipping those that form cycles.
  • Prim's Approach (This Lecture): Starts at a single arbitrary root vertex and grows the tree outward by always choosing the cheapest connecting edge.

The Original Graph

This is the graph provided on the whiteboard at the start of the lecture. Notice that it contains a loop on vertex F, and parallel edges between B and D.

Step 1 Pre-Processing

Before executing Prim's Algorithm, you must clean up the graph by applying two strict rules:

  • Remove all self-loops: A loop starts and ends at the same vertex. The instructor removes the loop on F (weight 1). You do not check its cost; loops are automatically discarded.
  • Remove parallel edges: If two vertices have multiple edges connecting them, keep the one with the lowest weight and remove the rest. Between B and D, we have weights 6 and 8. The instructor keeps the 6 and removes the 8.

Crucial Concept The Core Rule of Prim's

To grow the tree iteratively, you must choose a starting vertex. The instructor chooses vertex A.

⚠️ Instructor Warning: A Common Student Mistake! At every step, you must look at the outgoing edges from ALL vertices currently in your growing tree, not just the newest vertex you added. Failing to check the older vertices will result in a wrong MST!

You must select the edge with the minimum weight that connects a visited node to an unvisited node.

Step 2 Iterations 1 & 2

Iteration 1: Start at A. Available edges are A-B (7) and A-C (8). The minimum is 7. We choose A-B.

Iteration 2: Tree is {A, B}. We check ALL outgoing edges: A-C (8), B-C (3), B-D (6). The minimum is 3. We choose B-C.

Step 3 Iteration 3

Current Tree: {A, B, C}

We check edges from A, B, and C that lead to new nodes. Available edges: B-D (6), C-D (4), C-E (3). (Note: A-C is ignored because C is already visited).

The minimum is 3. We choose C-E.

Step 4 Iteration 4 (The Tie-Breaker)

Current Tree: {A, B, C, E}

Available edges leading to unvisited nodes (D, F): B-D (6), C-D (4), E-D (2), E-F (2).

💡 Tie-Breaker Rule There is a tie between E-D (2) and E-F (2). The instructor states you can choose either one arbitrarily. She decides to choose E-F (2).

Step 5 Final Iteration

Current Tree: {A, B, C, E, F}. Only vertex D is unvisited.

Available edges leading to D: B-D (6), C-D (4), E-D (2), F-D (5). The minimum is 2. We choose E-D.

All vertices are now included. The algorithm terminates.

Final Step Verifying the MST

At the end of the video, Jenny explains how to mathematically verify that the tree you drew is a valid Spanning Tree.

Let the original graph be G(V, E) and the spanning tree be G'(V', E').

  • Rule 1: Same number of Vertices. The MST must contain every vertex from the original graph.
    V' = V
    Check: Original graph has 6 vertices. MST has 6 vertices. (Pass)
  • Rule 2: Number of Edges. The MST must have exactly one less edge than the total number of vertices.
    |E'| = |V| - 1
    Check: 6 - 1 = 5 edges. Our tree has 5 edges. (Pass)
  • Rule 3: Subset of Edges. All edges in the MST must exist in the original graph.
    E' ⊆ E
  • Rule 4: Minimum Cost. The sum of the weights must be the lowest possible.
    Cost = 7 + 3 + 3 + 2 + 2 = 17
Final Note from Instructor: A single graph can have more than one valid Minimum Spanning Tree (because of tie-breakers like we saw in Step 4), but the total minimum weight will always be the same.
