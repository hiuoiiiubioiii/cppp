Binary Search Trees (BST)

Complete Notes: Core Rules, Insertion, and Deletion Methods

📘 1. The Core Rules of a BST

Before drawing any tree, you must understand the two strict rules that make a tree a Binary Search Tree.

Rule 1: The "Binary" Rule Every single node in the tree can have at most two children.
Valid child counts: 0, 1, or 2.
Rule 2: The "Search Tree" Rule
  • The Left Sub-tree of any node must contain values strictly LESS THAN that node.
  • The Right Sub-tree of any node must contain values strictly GREATER THAN that node.
Teacher's Emphasis: This rule applies to the ENTIRE sub-tree, not just the immediate left or right child! If the root is 11, you cannot have a 12 anywhere in the entire left branch, even if it's a grandchild.

🏗️ 2. Constructing a BST (Insertion)

Goal: Draw a BST by inserting the following numbers from left to right.

11 6 8 19 4 10 5 17 43 49 31

Step-by-Step Logic:

Always start searching for a placement from the Root node.

  • 11: First number, becomes the Root.
  • 6: 6 < 11. Goes to the Left.
  • 8: 8 < 11 (Left), but 8 > 6 (Right). Right child of 6.
  • 19: 19 > 11. Right child of 11.
  • 4: 4 < 11 (Left), 4 < 6 (Left). Left child of 6.
  • ...continue this pattern (always starting from 11 and traversing down).
          11
        /    \
      /        \
     6         19
   /  \       /  \
  4      8    17    43
  \      \        /  \
   5      10     31    49
Quick Test: Insert 60 Start at root (11). 60 > 11 (Right) → 60 > 19 (Right) → 60 > 43 (Right) → 60 > 49 (Right). 60 is placed as the right child of 49.

✂️ 3. Deletion (Cases 1 & 2)

Deleting from a BST introduces 3 distinct situations depending on how many children the target node has.

Situation 1: Deleting a node with 0 Children (Leaf Node)

Target: Delete 31.

  • Because 31 has no children, you simply erase it. No tree restructuring is required.

Situation 2: Deleting a node with 1 Child

Target: Delete 4.

  • Node 4 has exactly one child (Node 5).
  • Rule: Erase the target node and connect its parent directly to its single child. Node 5 shifts up to take Node 4's exact place.
    Before:              After deleting 4:
       6                        6  
     /  \                    /  \
    4      8                  5      8

     5 

🔥 4. Deletion: The Complex Case (2 Children)

Target: Delete 11 (The Root).

Because 11 has entire left and right sub-trees, we can't just delete it. We must replace it with a specific number from the existing tree. You have two valid methods.

Method A: In-Order Predecessor

The Predecessor is the Largest element in the Left Sub-tree.

  • Look at the entire left side of the tree (4, 5, 6, 8, 10).
  • The largest number is 10.
  • Replace 11 with 10. Erase the old 10.

Method B: In-Order Successor

The Successor is the Smallest element in the Right Sub-tree.

  • Look at the entire right side of the tree (17, 19, 31, 43, 49).
  • The smallest number is 17.
  • Replace 11 with 17. Erase the old 17.
Note: Both Method A and Method B result in perfectly valid Binary Search Trees. You can use either one on an exam.

💡 5. The "Cheat Code" for Predecessors & Successors

If looking at the visual tree is confusing, the instructor provides a foolproof method to find the replacement node.

If you write out the "In-order Traversal" of any valid BST, it will always result in a sorted (ascending) list of numbers.

4 5 6 8 10 11 17 19 31 43 49
  • Target to delete: 11
  • Predecessor: Look at the number immediately before it in the sorted list.
    → 10
  • Successor: Look at the number immediately after it in the sorted list.
    → 17
