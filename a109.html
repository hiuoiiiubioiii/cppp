1. The Initial Setup

Before calculations begin, the instructor establishes the working data on the whiteboard:

Keys to Insert 3, 2, 9, 6, 11, 13, 7, 12
Table Size (m) m = 10 (Indices 0 to 9)
Hash Function h(k) = 2k + 3

🔑 The Core Formula for Quadratic Probing

$$ \text{Location} = (u + i^2) \pmod m $$

Where:

  • \(u\) = Initial hash location calculated by \(h(k)\).
  • \(i\) = Probe attempt (ranges from \(0\) to \(m-1\)).
  • \(m\) = Size of the hash table.
Instructor’s Verbal Note: In Linear Probing, the formula was \((u + i) \pmod m\), meaning if there was a collision, you moved sequentially by 1 step. In Quadratic Probing, you jump forward using squares (\(i^2 = 0, 1, 4, 9, 16...\)) to avoid the "clustering" problem found in linear probing.

3. The First Collision

💥 Inserting Key: 11

\(u = h(11) = (2 \cdot 11 + 3) \pmod{10} = 25 \pmod{10} = 5\)

Action: Look at index 5. Collision! (6 is already there)

Applying Quadratic Formula: \((u + i^2) \pmod{10}\)

  • Probe 1 (\(i=0\)): \((5 + 0^2) \pmod{10} = 5\) (Occupied)
  • Probe 2 (\(i=1\)): \((5 + 1^2) \pmod{10} = 6\) (Free!)

Result: Store 11 at index 6. (Probes = 2)

5. The Major Collision

Instructor Note: This is the most complex insertion in the example. Pay close attention to the math here as we square higher values of \(i\).

🚨 Inserting Key: 12

\(u = h(12) = (24 + 3) \pmod{10} = 27 \pmod{10} = 7\)

Action: Look at index 7. Collision! (2 is there)

Applying Quadratic Formula:

  • Probe 1 (\(i=0\)): \((7 + 0^2) \pmod{10} = 7\) Occupied by 2
  • Probe 2 (\(i=1\)): \((7 + 1^2) \pmod{10} = 8\) Occupied by 7
  • Probe 3 (\(i=2\)): \((7 + 2^2) \pmod{10} = (7+4)\pmod{10} = 11 \pmod{10} = 1\) Occupied by 9
  • Probe 4 (\(i=3\)): \((7 + 3^2) \pmod{10} = (7+9)\pmod{10} = 16 \pmod{10} = 6\) Occupied by 11
  • Probe 5 (\(i=4\)): \((7 + 4^2) \pmod{10} = (7+16)\pmod{10} = 23 \pmod{10} = 3\) FREE!

Result: Store 12 at index 3. (Probes = 5)

Key Takeaway: Quadratic vs Linear Probing

She points out the specific insertion of Key 12. In Linear Probing, you would have just stepped down the array one by one looking for an open slot, which creates massive groups (clusters) of data.

By using Quadratic Probing (\(i^2\)), the jump distance increases exponentially (+1, +4, +9, +16), effectively breaking up the clustering problem and spreading the data out more evenly across the table!
