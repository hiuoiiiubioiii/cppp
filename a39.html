Binary Expression Trees

Complete Video Breakdown & Step-by-Step Visualizations

1. What is an Expression Tree?

An expression tree is a specific kind of binary tree used to represent mathematical or logical expressions. These expressions consist of operands (variables or constants) and operators.

The Golden Rules of the Tree Structure:
  • Leaves (External Nodes): Must always contain Operands (e.g., a, b, c, x, y).
  • Internal Nodes: Must always contain Operators (e.g., +, -, *, /).

While a common method is to convert an infix expression to postfix first and use a stack, this lecture focuses on a direct method: building the tree directly from an Infix expression without brackets.

2. Precedence and Associativity Rules

To build the tree correctly, you must strictly follow the rules of Operator Precedence and Associativity. The instructor writes this exact table on the whiteboard:

Operator Operation Precedence Associativity
^ Power / Exponent Highest Right to Left (R-L)
* , / Multiply / Divide Medium Left to Right (L-R)
+ , - Add / Subtract Lowest Left to Right (L-R)
Crucial Concept: Finding the Root
How do you know which operator forms the root node? A tree is evaluated from the bottom (leaves) to the top (root). Therefore, the operator that is evaluated LAST in the mathematical equation becomes the root of the tree. Look for the operator with the least precedence.

3. Step-by-Step Tree Construction

We are going to construct a binary expression tree from the following Infix expression:

a * b / c + e / f * g + k - x * y

Step 1: Find the Main Root

Scan the equation for the lowest precedence operators. We find +, +, and -. Because associativity for these is Left-to-Right, the very last operation to be evaluated is the - at the far right. Therefore, - becomes our main root.

                Root --> Right

Step 2: Build the Right Subtree

Looking at the right side x * y, there is only one operator: *. That becomes the node, with operands x and y as its leaves.

Step 3: Break Down the Left Subtree

Now look at a * b / c + e / f * g + k. The lowest precedence operators are the two + signs. Because of Left-to-Right associativity, the second + is evaluated last. It becomes the root of this sub-tree.

                Root --> M1
                P1 --> Left2
                P1 --> K
                M1 --> X
                M1 --> Y

Step 4: Continuing Left

We are left with a * b / c + e / f * g. There is only one low-precedence operator here: the +. This splits our remaining equation down the middle.

            graph TD
                Root(( - ))
                P1(( + ))
                M1(( * ))
                P2(( + ))
                K(( k ))
                Left3["a * b / c"]
                Right3["e / f * g"]
                X(( x ))
                Y(( y ))
                
                Root --> P1
                Root --> M1
                P1 --> P2
                P1 --> K
                P2 --> Left3
                P2 --> Right3
                M1 --> X
                M1 --> Y

Step 5: Solving a * b / c

Here, * and / have equal precedence. Associativity is Left-to-Right. Therefore, the division (/) happens last. / is the parent node, c is the right leaf, and a * b forms a subtree on the left.

Step 6: Solving e / f * g

Similarly, / and * tie. Left-to-Right associativity means the multiplication (*) happens last. * is the parent node, g is the right leaf, and e / f forms a subtree on the left.

4. Deriving Prefix and Postfix Expressions

Once you have constructed the binary expression tree, you don't need a complex algorithm to find the Prefix or Postfix notations. You simply perform basic tree traversals.

  • Inorder Traversal (Left, Root, Right): Gives you the original Infix expression (use this to check your work!).
  • Preorder Traversal (Root, Left, Right): Gives you the Prefix expression. (Root comes Pre/First)
  • Postorder Traversal (Left, Right, Root): Gives you the Postfix expression. (Root comes Post/Last)
