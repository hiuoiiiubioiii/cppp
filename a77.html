1. The Core Rules & Formulas

Before inserting numbers, we must establish the boundaries of our B+ Tree. The instructor writes the following formulas on the board based on the given order.

Given Order (m) = 4

  • Max Children (Pointers): m = 4
  • Min Children: ⌈m/2rceil = 2
  • Max Keys (Data per node): m - 1 = 3 (Crucial: A node can only hold 3 numbers!)
  • Min Keys: ⌈m/2rceil - 1 = 1

Dataset to Insert:

1, 4, 7, 10, 17, 21, 31, 25, 19, 20, 28, 42

2. B-Tree vs. B+ Tree Properties

The instructor highlights three major theoretical differences that govern how we build this tree:

  • Data Location: In a B+ Tree, the actual data is ONLY stored in the leaf nodes (the bottom row).
  • Internal Nodes: The internal (parent) nodes act purely as "guideposts" or indexes to direct the search. They do not hold actual records.
  • The Linked List: All leaf nodes are connected to each other sequentially via a linked list. This allows for rapid range queries.
The "Right-Biased" Splitting Rule:
When a node overflows (gets 4 elements), we must split it. Because 4 is an even number, the middle could be the 2nd or 3rd element. The instructor uses a Right-Biased approach, choosing the 3rd element to move up.

Instructor's Final Takeaways

  • Look at the bottom row: Every single number from the original dataset exists in the leaf nodes. No data is missing.
  • Look at the top rows: The numbers here (20, 7, 17, 25, 31) are simply duplicates used to guide the search algorithm.
  • Sequential Access: If you need to print all data in ascending order, you don't need to traverse up and down the tree. You just find the leftmost node `[1, 4]` and follow the → pointers all the way to `[31, 42]`.
