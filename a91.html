Finding All Bridges in a Graph

In-depth video breakdown & whiteboard notes • Scroll to begin

Concept

What exactly is a "Bridge"?

An edge in a graph is considered a bridge if removing it increases the number of connected components in the graph. In simpler terms: if you cut this edge, does the graph split into disconnected pieces?

In her whiteboard example (0:22), the instructor shows a graph. If we remove the edge between d and e, the graph splits. If we remove the edge between e and f, it splits again. These structural weak points are the bridges.

The Strategy

DFS Traversal & Tracking Time

To find bridges efficiently in large graphs, we use a Depth-First Search (DFS). As we traverse, the instructor writes a fraction next to every node on the whiteboard tracking two crucial numbers:

  • Discovery Time (dt): The chronological step when we first visit a node (1, 2, 3...).
  • Lowest Reachable Time (low): The lowest "Discovery Time" this node can reach by looking at its neighbors (excluding the parent node it just came from).

On the whiteboard, it looks like this: Discovery Time / Lowest Time (e.g., 6 / 4).

Core Rule

The Golden Rule of Bridges

As you hit a dead end in the graph and start backtracking up the DFS tree, a child node reports its Lowest Reachable Time back to its parent.

🚨 The Bridge Formula

If an edge connects a Parent to a Child, it is a bridge IF AND ONLY IF:

Lowest_Time [Child] > Discovery_Time [Parent]

Why? Because if the child's lowest reachable time is strictly greater than the parent's discovery time, it means the child has NO back-edges (alternate routes) connecting it to older parts of the graph. The ONLY way back is through the parent. Cut the parent, and the child breaks off.

Walkthrough Step 1

Forward Traversal & Initial Backtracking

Starting at node d, the DFS dives deep, assigning discovery times:

d(1) ➔ f(2) ➔ g(3) ➔ c(4) ➔ b(5) ➔ a(6)

At a, we hit a dead end and begin backtracking and calculating "Lowest Reachable Times":

  • At Node a (dt=6): Neighbors are b (parent) and c (dt=4). Ignoring the parent, it can reach c. Its lowest time is 4. She writes 6 / 4. Backtracks to b. Check: 4 < 5. Not a bridge.
  • At Node b (dt=5): DFS branches out to e (dt=7). e can reach d(1). So e is 7 / 1. e backtracks to b. Check: 1 < 5. Not a bridge.
  • Node b adopts e's lowest time, becoming 5 / 1, and backtracks to c. Check: 1 < 4. Not a bridge.
Walkthrough Step 2

Traversing the Bottom Half & Bridge #1

From node c(4), the DFS travels down into the lower half of the whiteboard graph:

c ➔ h(8) ➔ i(9) ➔ k(10) ➔ l(11) ➔ j(12)

After backtracking up through j and l, node k(10) branches down again to the final cluster:

k(10) ➔ m(13) ➔ n(14) ➔ o(15)

Applying the Math at Node m (13:35):

  • Node m completes its check of n and o. They form a cycle, so m cannot reach anything older than itself. Its lowest time is 13.
  • m backtracks to its parent, k.
  • The Check: Lowest [m] (13) > Discovery [k] (10).
  • Result: Because 13 is greater than 10, the edge k-m is our first bridge! She circles this on the board.
Walkthrough Step 3

Backtracking & Bridge #2

The DFS continues to backtrack up the left side of the graph: k ➔ i ➔ h.

  • k passes up a lowest time of 8.
  • i passes up a lowest time of 8.
  • Node h receives 8. It has no other edges. So h's lowest time remains 8.

Applying the Math at Node h (14:40):

  • Node h backtracks to its parent, c.
  • The Check: Lowest [h] (8) > Discovery [c] (4).
  • Result: Because 8 is strictly greater than 4, the edge c-h is our second bridge. Removing it separates the entire top and bottom halves of the whiteboard graph.
Conclusion

Time Complexity

After backtracking all the way to the start, we conclude the graph contains exactly two bridges.

At 19:20 in the video, the instructor explains the time complexity of this algorithm:

Time Complexity: Θ(V + E)

Where V is Vertices (Nodes) and E is Edges.

Why? In the worst-case scenario, the Depth-First Search algorithm requires us to visit every single vertex to assign discovery times, and traverse across every single edge exactly once to perform the backtracking lowest-time checks.
