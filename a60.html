Construct BST from Given Postorder

Complete Video Breakdown & Step-by-Step Notes

1. The Fundamentals (Whiteboard Top Right)

Before solving the problem, the instructor writes down the crucial rules for tree traversals.

  • Postorder: (Left, Right, Root) 👉 The Root is ALWAYS at the last position.
  • Inorder: (Left, Root, Right) 👉 The Root is in the middle.
  • Preorder: (Root, Left, Right) 👉 The Root is at the first position.
"As you know, the property of a Binary Search Tree (BST) is: when you find out the Inorder traversal of a BST, that Inorder traversal would always be in ascending order. That is for sure."

Key Strategy: We use Postorder to find the ROOT (scanning right to left). We use Inorder to figure out who goes in the LEFT subtree and who goes in the RIGHT subtree.

3. Building the Tree (Level 1)

Finding the Main Root: Look at the Postorder array. The right-most element is 20. Therefore, 20 is the root of the entire tree.

Splitting the tree: Go to the Inorder array and locate 20. Everything to the left of 20 goes to the left subtree. Everything to the right goes to the right subtree.

20
Left Subtree Elements:
{5, 16, 17, 18, 19}
Right Subtree Elements:
{60, 70, 85}
"Now the problem has been divided into two parts. You can construct either the left subtree first or the right subtree first. It's up to you. I am going to construct the left subtree first."

4. Building the Left Subtree

We need to find the root among {5, 16, 17, 18, 19}.

Rule: Scan the Postorder array from Right to Left. Whichever of these numbers appears first is the root of this subtree.

Postorder: 5, 17, 19, 18, 16, 70, 85, 60, 20.

Reading right to left, skipping 70, 85, 60... we hit 16 first. So, 16 is the root!

Go to the Inorder array: 5, 16, 17, 18, 19.

  • Left of 16 is {5}.
  • Right of 16 is {17, 18, 19}.
16
Left:
{5}
Right:
{17, 18, 19}

(Since 5 is a single element, it becomes a leaf node automatically).

Resolving {17, 18, 19}

Scan Postorder right-to-left looking for 17, 18, or 19.
Postorder: 5, 17, 19, 18, 16...

We hit 18 first! So 18 is the root.
Inorder split: 17, 18, 19.
17 goes to the left of 18. 19 goes to the right of 18.

5. Building the Right Subtree

Now we go back up to the right side of 20. The elements are {60, 70, 85}.

Scan Postorder right-to-left: 5, 17, 19, 18, 16, 70, 85, 60, 20.

We hit 60 first! So 60 is the root.

Go to the Inorder array: 60, 70, 85.

  • Left of 60: Nothing!
  • Right of 60: {70, 85}. Both go to the right.

Resolving {70, 85}

Scan Postorder right-to-left looking for 70 or 85: ... 70, 85, 60...

We hit 85 first! 85 is the root.
Inorder split: 70, 85.
70 is to the left of 85, so it becomes the left child. Right child is Null.

💡 7. Pro-Tip: The Direct Method (Without Inorder)

At the very end of the video, the instructor explains a faster method for exams if you don't want to write out the Inorder array every time.

"Without Inorder also you can construct the binary search tree. Just trace out this Postorder from right to left..."
  1. Start at right end of Postorder: 20. This is the root.
  2. Next is 60: Because it is a BST, compare 60 to 20. 60 > 20, so it goes to the Right.
  3. Next is 85: Compare from root. 85 > 20 (go right). 85 > 60 (go right). 85 is right child of 60.
  4. Next is 70: 70 > 20 (go right). 70 > 60 (go right). 70 < 85 (go left). 70 is left child of 85.
  5. Next is 16: 16 < 20 (go left). 16 is left child of 20.
  6. ...and so on!

Why this works: The nature of a Binary Search Tree dictates that all left children must be smaller than the parent, and right children must be greater. Reading Postorder backwards essentially gives you the nodes top-down, right-to-left.
